measures her results

After execution, ρ needs to evaluate how well her circuit performed. Three metrics capture different aspects:

Hellinger Fidelity (HF)
Measures overlap between ideal and observed distributions
$HF = \left(\sum_i \sqrt{p_i \cdot q_i}\right)^2$
HF = 1 → perfect match
Total Variation Distance (TVD)
Measures maximum divergence between distributions
$TVD = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i |p_i - q_i|$
TVD = 0 → identical
DSR (ours)
Differential Success Rate — our contribution
Distribution-aware, clips noise
We'll discuss DSR in detail later

We use regression models to predict these post-runtime values from pre-runtime features

Problem Statement

Research Question

To what extent can a compact set of pre-runtime metrics, the "circuit footprint," serve as a reliable predictor for the quality of quantum software across different algorithm paradigms and diverse quantum service providers?

Hypotheses

H1 — Primary Hypothesis
A compact "footprint," constructed from a quantum circuit's pre-runtime metrics, contains sufficient information to estimate the execution quality of a quantum algorithm on a quantum device, when validated against ground-truth data from hardware execution.
H2 — Scalability Consistency
Models trained on instances of the selected primitives (QFT, GO, BV, vTP, GHZ) generalize to predict the quality metrics of unobserved circuit configurations within the same algorithmic family.
H3 — Cross-Provider Transferability
A single predictive model trained using the circuit footprint generalizes across different quantum providers.

Objectives

  • O1 — Circuit Footprint Definition: Identify the most relevant pre-runtime metrics that define a "circuit footprint" capable of discriminating between algorithmic paradigms.
  • O2 — Predictive Modeling: Train ML models to map the footprint to post-runtime quality (DSR, HF, TVD) across providers.
  • O3 — Validation: Evaluate generalizability against ground-truth from real hardware, using unobserved circuit configurations within the same algorithmic families.
