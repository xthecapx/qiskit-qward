\begin{abstract}

A fundamental requirement in software engineering is the ability to validate that a program execution produced the intended result. In classical computing, the deterministic nature of execution enables direct validation by comparing the observed output against an expected value. In contrast, quantum computing (QC) is inherently stochastic, so the outcome of an execution is a probability distribution rather than a single deterministic value. This shifts validation from checking a single output to evaluating whether the expected outcome(s) stand out prominently in the observed distribution. To address this need, this paper presents the \emph{Differential Success Rate (DSR)}, a metric designed to improve the interpretability of quantum algorithm runs by quantifying how clearly the expected outcome(s) can be distinguished from the remaining observed outcomes.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ensuring successful execution of a program is a key requirement in software engineering. In classical computing, this process is done by directly comparing the observed output against an expected value. In other words, each execution produces a reproducible output. However, this deterministic validation model is not applicable in the QC realm, since in order to take advantage of quantum mechanics, it is required to run an algorithm multiple times and then analyze the resulting distribution to validate the execution success.

In particular, a single run of a quantum algorithm produces a single classical state \cite{nielsenQuantumComputationQuantuma_2010}. Yet, to take advantage of quantum phenomena such as entanglement, superposition, and interference, it is necessary to construct a probability distribution over all possible outcomes by running the algorithm multiple times. Today, a histogram is typically used to represent this output, where the x-axis corresponds to the possible bit-string outcomes, and the y-axis counts the frequency of the observed values. By contrast, in hardware based on quantum annealing \cite{Hauke_2020_2020}, the output corresponds to the physical configuration of the system (a specific bit string) that represents the ground state (or a low-energy configuration). Another alternative to histograms is seen in variational algorithms \cite{McClean_2016_2016}, where quantum hardware outputs a real scalar value (the expected value of a cost function, such as energy), which is used as input to a classical optimizer to finally produce a bit-string result.

The probability distributions described above are accessible through the SDKs or APIs provided by the quantum hardware vendors, most of which use superconducting qubits for the built-in quantum processing unit (QPU). A common feature of such SDKs is the ability to compute histograms of execution outcomes. This motivates the creation of a metric that quantifies how distinctly the expected outcomes stand out within the observed distribution.

Before introducing such a metric, it is worth considering the applicability of classical performance metrics in quantum software; although doing so restricts the evaluation of quantum success to a classical paradigm in which the observed outcomes are fundamentally stochastic. More precisely, classical success measures can be defined at the shot level, but, because quantum processes require repeated executions to obtain meaningful results, the success measure should be formulated at the job level and, in some scenarios, even at the batch level.

Several metrics have been proposed to quantify the successful execution of quantum circuits \cite{mohapatraBenchmarkingFidelityMetrics_2025}; however, most of them are designed to characterize the fidelity of the quantum hardware rather than to evaluate how distinctly a target outcome emerges in the observed distribution. Section~\ref{sec:related-work} explores these metrics in detail and positions the DSR relative to them.

In this paper, we introduce the differential success rate (DSR), a quantitative metric designed to measure the level of distinction of a target quantum state within a given histogram. The DSR is expressed as a percentage, thereby serving as an indicator of how clearly the expected quantum state emerges relative to the observed probability distribution generated by current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices.

To characterize the behavior of DSR, we first execute the Grover and quantum Fourier transform algorithms using the AER simulator parameterized with the hardware calibration values reported for QPUs commonly available from major quantum service providers. The DSR metric is then integrated into qWard, a Python-based framework for the analysis of quantum circuits. Finally, we run the corresponding circuits on the physical QPUs \textit{ibm\_fez}, \textit{ibm\_torino}, and \textit{ibm\_marrakesh} accessed via the IBM Quantum service, as well as on the Rigetti Ankaa-3 device accessed via Amazon Web Services (AWS).

Our experiments demonstrate the usefulness of the DSR metric for improving the interpretability of quantum jobs by defining clear ranges of success for the Grover and QFT algorithms, on two quantum providers (4 different QPUs). Such intervals were determined by systematically scaling and executing the algorithms, computing the DSR, and correlating its values with the corresponding number of qubits and the circuit depth. In addition, we empirically compare DSR against Hellinger fidelity and total variation distance fidelity, showing that the metrics capture complementary aspects of algorithm success.

Section~\ref{sec:measuring-success} introduces the execution model and success criteria, while Section~\ref{sec:dsr} describes the DSR metric. Section~\ref{subsec:experiment} describes the experiment setup, and Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the empirical results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

This study builds on two previous studies \cite{qward2026, vTPMarquez_2026}. The first is the qWard project, a Python-based framework library designed for the analysis of quantum circuits and their executions. The second study presents our initial investigation into the execution success of quantum algorithms, as reported in IEEE Access in early 2026. In that work, we introduced a variation of the quantum teleportation protocol and proposed a systematic methodology to evaluate its success.

With qWard, our aim was to describe quantum algorithms through a set of quantitative metrics computed prior to algorithm execution (designated as pre-runtime metrics). Following the execution of the algorithm, qWard further provides tools to analyze the resulting probability distribution by using a set of post-runtime metrics, with the aim of facilitating the identification of correlations between pre-runtime and post-runtime characteristics.

To provide evidence for a correlation between pre-runtime and post-runtime metrics, we conducted a follow-up study examining how these metrics evolve under a variation of the teleportation protocol. Specifically, we first prepare a quantum circuit to transmit a message and then apply its transposed version with the objective of reconstructing the original input state before applying the measurement operation. In this experiment, the success function quantifies the number of instances in which the algorithm correctly reconstructs the initial state.

Based on the findings of the two previous studies, we developed DSR as a post-runtime metric. These results allowed us to establish the necessary tooling for our quantum development workflow, in which we compute pre-runtime metrics, run the quantum algorithms, and finally compute post-runtime metrics in order to determine the success rate.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related-work}

In this section, we examine several standard metrics employed to quantify the successful execution of quantum algorithms and compare them with the DSR metric.

\subsection{Density-Matrix Based Fidelity}
\label{sec:sec:desity-matrix}

The validation of a quantum program via the density matrix constitutes a natural first step towards characterizing algorithm execution, and was presented in detail by Uhlmann in \cite{UHLMANN1976273_1976}. In summary, the density matrix fidelity (DM fidelity) process starts by calculating the theoretical value of the quantum operation. Then, the resulting density matrix is derived to compare it with the theoretical expectation and finally determine the discrepancy between the two values.

Although DM fidelity is a robust way to validate the execution of a quantum algorithm, from the perspective of software engineering, it is highly impractical \cite{LI20146878_2014}. First, the QPU does not directly produce a density matrix; instead, it outputs classical bit strings. Reconstructing the density matrix would require performing full quantum state tomography, an operation that is computationally expensive, thereby making the procedure computationally infeasible in practice. Second, even though it is possible to generate the ideal state by using a simulator (for instance, AER), the size of the matrices grows exponentially with the number of qubits, so it is impossible to compute the ideal density matrix using classical resources. In other words, the target density matrix for practical applications is fundamentally unknown.

\subsection{Estimated Success Probability}

A typical way to evaluate how successfully a program runs is to estimate the success rate associated with each component involved in its operation. In the context of QC, this can be achieved by combining the success probabilities of individual operations (such as gates and measurements). During the calibration of a QPU, a quantum provider reports the error rate associated with each operation; therefore, the ESP is obtained by computing the success rate of each element (i.e. \(1 - \text{error}\)) and then taking the product of these success rates over all the circuit operations.

Although ESP is a useful metric for measuring the success of a given hardware, it provides limited insight into the behavior of specific algorithms, since it assumes that every operation affects the output independently. Furthermore, Mohapatra et al. (2025) report that ESP performs reliably only for circuits with depth below 50. Thus, since real-world applications require circuits of much greater depth, the ESP becomes inaccurate for such use cases; instead, metrics such as Probability of Successful Trials (PST) or quantum vulnerability analysis (QVA) \cite{qiQuantumVulnerabilityAnalysis_2024} are preferred.

\subsection{Probability of Successful Trials}

Probability of successful trials (PST) \cite{mohapatraBenchmarkingFidelityMetrics_2025} is a method to measure the successful execution of a quantum circuit. It exploits the property that multiplying a unitary matrix by its conjugate transpose (in either order) produces the identity matrix. The core idea is to engineer the circuit so that its output quantum state approximates its initial state, and then define the metric as the ratio of the number of initial states obtained as an output to the total number of trials.

The main limitation of PST is that its definition modifies the structure of the original circuit. Since calculating PST requires the circuit and its inverse, the total depth of the circuit is doubled, thereby amplifying the impact of errors. In addition, standard PST employs a binary classification for success; because it only checks if the output matches the expected initial state (e.g., an all-zero output), an outcome with a single bit-flip error is penalized in the exact same way as one in which every bit is corrupted.

\subsection{Hellinger Fidelity}

Hellinger fidelity is a metric recommended for algorithms like the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) \cite{zhouQuantumApproximateOptimization_2020}, where the ideal solution is a probability distribution spread over many different basis states. Hellinger fidelity is calculated by comparing the expected distribution with the returned outcome distribution, returning a 1 if both are identical or 0 if there is no overlap.

The major limitation of Hellinger fidelity \cite{mohapatraBenchmarkingFidelityMetrics_2025} lies in the high computational cost of calculating the expected probability distribution. In addition, there is a mathematical blind spot for circuits targeting a small set of outcomes (e.g., the preparation of a Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger state), since small errors do not alter the score sufficiently to reflect the effect of the noise. Furthermore, this metric cannot capture phase-related errors because its evaluation is based exclusively on the analysis of probability distributions, where phase information has already been lost due to the measurement operation.

\subsection{Total Variation Distance}

The total variation distance (TVD) \cite{knillBoundsApproximationTotal_1995, bhattacharyyaApproximatingTotalVariation_2023} is a metric that shares similarities with Hellinger fidelity. Both metrics are particularly suitable for algorithms whose ideal output is a probability distribution with values spread over many states. What makes TVD unique is its mathematical approach to histogram comparison, which is defined as half the sum of the absolute differences between the corresponding probabilities. By definition, a TVD value of 0 indicates identical distributions, while a value of 1 means completely different. However, for consistency with other fidelity metrics, TVD is often reported as $1 - TVD$.

Similarly, TVD shares the limitations of Hellinger fidelity: a high computational overhead due to the need to estimate the ideal probability distribution, a limited sensitivity to errors in circuits whose ideal output is concentrated on a small number of dominant states, and a fundamental inability to capture information about phase-related errors.

\section{Execution model and success criteria}
\label{sec:measuring-success}

The quantum software development lifecycle has been the subject of growing attention in the software engineering community. Zhao \cite{zhaoQuantumSoftwareEngineering_2020} identified key challenges that distinguish QC from classical development. Weder et al. \cite{wederQuantumSoftwareDevelopment_2022} presented a lifecycle for hybrid quantum-classical applications. And more recently, Khan et al. \cite{khanAdvancingQuantumSoftware_2024} proposed a fullstack model for QC. Based on those studies and on our experience developing qWard, we organize the QC process into three phases: the development phase, the execution phase, and the analysis phase.

During the development phase, the developer defines the target quantum state and prepares an algorithm to achieve it. An important aspect of the development phase is implementing a reduced version of the algorithm that can be run on quantum simulators (using classical hardware) before building the full-scale version intended for execution on a QPU. In addition, it is common practice to employ some of the well-defined algorithms as black boxes, which, once properly linked, produce the desired output.

During the execution phase, the developer plans and executes a job on the QPU. An important task at this stage is to specify how many times the job will run (the number of shots). This choice is directly linked to the success rate of the quantum operations, since the lower the success rate, the more executions are required. At this stage, the algorithm must be transpiled into the supported operations of the chosen quantum provider. This step also involves using a simulator to verify that the resulting circuit performs as intended. Finally, with both the execution plan and the transpiled circuits ready, the developer runs a quick benchmark of the algorithm on the real QPU. This step helps build confidence in the execution and provides an estimate of the required budget of the experiment.

During the analysis phase, the developer pulls data from the quantum provider until the job is reported as complete. The quantum provider returns multiple bit strings that are ultimately consolidated into a histogram. With all responses ready, the goal now is to determine whether the response was successful. It is important to note that during the development phase, an initial version of the analysis is produced, but the analysis is often refined later, once the real data are available.

The main goal of DSR is to facilitate the interpretation of quantum algorithm results and to act as a low-cost validation step at every stage of the development lifecycle. Because it depends on the histogram and the expected quantum state, this metric is meant to be applied in all the development phases described above whenever both requirements are met.

\section{Differential Success Rate}
\label{sec:dsr}

The DSR is defined as a metric that quantifies how well a target quantum state can be distinguished from the other values in a histogram. The DSR utilizes the Michelson contrast formulation \cite{michelsonInterferenceMethods_1890, michelsonStudiesOptics_1927} as it provides three characteristics that are desirable for validating QC algorithms: (i) scale invariance, since the DSR does not depend on the total number of shots, it captures the shape of the distribution rather than the size of the sampling budget; (ii) a bounded range in $[0, 1]$, to follow the conventions of the fidelity metrics; and (iii) sensitivity to the relative distinction between the expected outcome and the next strongest peak, rather than to the absolute success probability.

Although DSR eliminates the need for computationally expensive simulations of the ideal probability distribution, it still inherits the limitations of existing metrics derived from algorithm outcomes. These include the inability to capture information about phase-related errors and the restriction to problems for which the target state is known.

\subsection{Definitions}

Let $\mathcal{C} : \{0,1\}^n \to \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ denote the histogram returned by a quantum job, defined as a mapping of each $n$-bit string $x$ to its observation count $\mathcal{C}(x)$. The total number of shots is $S = \sum_{x} \mathcal{C}(x)$, and the probability of each outcome is estimated as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p_x}
    p_{x} = \frac{\mathcal{C}(x)}{S}
\end{equation}

Let us now denote by $E$ the \emph{set of desired outcomes}, which represent the bit strings that the algorithm is intended to generate (e.g. the marked states in Grover’s algorithm) and define $K = |E|$ as its cardinality.

Based on these definitions, Equation~\eqref{eq:p_exp} represents the total probability mass associated with the expected results, Equation~\eqref{eq:p_exp_bar} denotes the average probability per expected outcome, and Equation~\eqref{eq:p_comp} specifies the probability of the strongest competing peak (i.e., the highest peak outside~$E$).

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p_exp}
    p_{\text{exp}} = \sum_{x \in E} p_x
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p_exp_bar}
    \bar{p}_{\text{exp}} = \frac{p_{\text{exp}}}{K}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p_comp}
    p_{\text{comp}} = \max_{x \notin E}\; p_x
\end{equation}

Observe that when $S = 0$, the probability estimates $p_x$ are \emph{undefined} due to Equation~\eqref{eq:p_x}, and when $E$ is empty, $K = 0$ renders Equation~\eqref{eq:p_exp_bar} \emph{undefined}; in both cases the DSR is reported as 0. In contrast, if all observed states reside in $E$, then the set $\{x \notin E\}$ is \emph{empty}, and Equation~\eqref{eq:p_comp} has no terms. By convention, we set $p_{\text{comp}} = 0$, reflecting the absence of any competing outcome; substituting in Equation~\eqref{eq:dsr} results in $\mathrm{DSR} = \bar{p}_{\text{exp}}\,/\,\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} = 1$ (given $\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} > 0$).

\subsection{Metric}

Applying the Michelson contrast to $\bar{p}_{\text{exp}}$ and $p_{\text{comp}}$, the DSR is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dsr}
\mathrm{DSR} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
\frac{\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} - p_{\text{comp}}}
     {\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} + p_{\text{comp}}},\; 0,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}

where $\mathrm{clip}(v, 0, 1) = \max(0, \min(1, v))$. Since $\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} \geq 0$ and $p_{\text{comp}} \geq 0$, the fraction is already bounded in $[-1, 1]$; therefore, the clip function affects only the cases where $p_{\text{comp}} > \bar{p}_{\text{exp}}$, which sets the result at 0. This is an intentional design choice in which, when the strongest competitor dominates the expected outcome, the DSR reports no distinction, rather than a negative score.

To conclude this section, a DSR of 0 indicates that the expected outcome is no more frequent than the peak of the strongest competitor in the histogram (indistinguishable from noise), while a DSR of 1 indicates that the expected outcome dominates. In practical terms, if $\mathrm{DSR} = d$ (for $d < 1$), the signal-to-competitor ratio satisfies $\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} / p_{\text{comp}} = (1 + d)\,/\,(1 - d)$; for example, $d = 0.8$ corresponds to a ratio $9{:}1$.

\subsection{Alternative formulations}

Beyond the Michelson expression provided in Equation~\eqref{eq:dsr}, we analyzed three additional contrast functions using the collected data set (refer to Section~\ref{subsec:experiment} for details). The goal was to benchmark all formulations to ultimately identify the most suitable option. Table~\ref{tab:dsr_variants} reports the candidate formulations considered.

\begin{table}[ht!]
\centering
\caption{DSR variant formulations.}
\label{tab:dsr_variants}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Equation} & \textbf{Range} \\
\hline
Michelson       & Eq.~\eqref{eq:dsr}       & $[0,\,1]$ \\
Ratio           & Eq.~\eqref{eq:dsr_ratio}  & $[0.5,\,1]$ \\
Log-Ratio       & Eq.~\eqref{eq:dsr_log}    & $[0.5,\,1]$ \\
Norm-Margin     & Eq.~\eqref{eq:dsr_nm}     & $[0,\,1]$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The \emph{ratio} variant normalizes the expected probability by the total probability of both peaks:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dsr_ratio}
\mathrm{DSR}_{\text{ratio}} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
\frac{\bar{p}_{\text{exp}}}
     {\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} + p_{\text{comp}}},\; 0,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}

The \emph{log-ratio} variant applies a sigmoid function $\sigma(z)=1/(1+e^{-z})$ to the logarithmic ratio of the two peaks, mapping the result to the interval $[0,1]$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dsr_log}
\mathrm{DSR}_{\text{log}} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
\sigma\!\left(\ln \frac{\bar{p}_{\text{exp}}}{p_{\text{comp}}}\right),\; 0,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}

The \emph{normalized margin} variant divides the difference between the two peaks by the larger of the two:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dsr_nm}
\mathrm{DSR}_{\text{nm}} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
\frac{\bar{p}_{\text{exp}} - p_{\text{comp}}}
     {\max(\bar{p}_{\text{exp}},\, p_{\text{comp}})},\; 0,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/1_grover_dsr_variants_qubits.png}
  \caption{DSR variants for the Grover algorithm as a function of the number of qubits (IBM backends).}
  \label{fig:dsr_variants}
\end{figure}

The pattern shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dsr_variants} was consistent across all three algorithms evaluated in the experiment described in Section~\ref{subsec:experiment}; however, only the Grover results are shown due to space limitations. The figure reports the median DSR of each variant as a function of the number of qubits, using the highest level of transpilation optimization.

Both the Michelson (teal line) and the normalized-margin (pink line) variants decrease monotonically and reach~$0$ at six qubits; however, Michelson drops more sharply in the $3$--$5$ qubit range, providing an earlier indication of performance degradation. The log-ratio (purple line) and ratio (orange line) variants exhibit a slower decline and, crucially, still report values of approximately $0.2$--$0.4$ at six to eight qubits, where the expected outcome no longer stands out in the histogram. This behavior masks the complete algorithm failure and reduces the ability of the metric to discriminate between partial and total loss of signal.

Based on this analysis, we select the Michelson formulation to compute the DSR. In summary, The Michelson formulation is defined in the interval $[0,1]$, is also the first to indicate failure, and thus is the most sensitive to performance degradation.

\section{Experiment setup}
\label{subsec:experiment}

To demonstrate the functionality of DSR, we designed a set of experiments based on Grover’s search algorithm and the quantum Fourier transform (QFT). These algorithms were selected because they operate on quantum states that are well defined and analytically tractable as they grow, thereby enabling rigorous characterization of the DSR performance.

To test Grover's search algorithm, we created different configurations of the circuit in which we defined a set of marked states. The algorithm amplifies the probability of measuring those states before the measurement operation is applied. A successful execution of the Grover search is one in which the marked states predominate in the histogram.

To evaluate the QFT algorithm, we set the algorithm in two ways. In the first case, \emph{round-trip} mode, the circuit applies the QFT followed by its inverse, so that a correct execution returns the original input state (similar to what we describe in SubSection~\ref{sec:sec:desity-matrix}). In the second configuration, \emph{period-detection} mode, the circuit encodes a signal with a known period~$p$ on $n$~qubits and then applies the QFT. The transform reveals the frequency content of the signal, producing peaks at the positions $k \cdot N/p$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, p{-}1$, where $N = 2^n$.

For the Grover algorithm, the \emph{scalability} study (S2--S14) uses a single marked state while growing the number of qubits from 2 to 14, expanding the bit-length of the marked state (e.g., $\ket{01}$ for 2~qubits, $\ket{0110}$ for 4~qubits, $\ket{01100110}$ for 8~qubits). The \emph{marked-count} study (M3, M4) fixes the number of qubits at 3 and 4, respectively, and varies the number of marked states (1, 2, and 4) in order to assess whether the size of~$E$ influences the metric. The \emph{Hamming-weight} study (H3, H4) varies the Hamming weight (i.e., the number of ones) of the marked state for 3- and 4-qubit systems. Finally, the \emph{symmetry} study (SYM, ASYM) fixes the number of qubits to 3 and evaluates whether the symmetry of the marked states affects the output.

For the QFT algorithm, the \emph{round-trip scalability} study (SR2--SR10) grows the register from~2 to~10 qubits, extending the input state (e.g., $\ket{01}$ for 2~qubits, $\ket{0110}$ for 4~qubits, $\ket{01100110}$ for 8~qubits). The \emph{period-detection scalability} study (SP3--SP6) increases the qubit count from~3 to~6 while fixing the period, so that the number of possible output states grows, but the number of expected peaks remains constant. The \emph{period-variation} study (PV4, PV6) does the opposite, fixing the qubit count at~4 or~6 and varying the period, which increases the number of expected peaks and makes them harder to resolve. The \emph{input-variation} study (IV4) fixes the qubit count at~4 in round-trip mode and tests five different input states ($\ket{0000}$, $\ket{1111}$, $\ket{0101}$, $\ket{1010}$, $\ket{0001}$).

\subsection{Backends and execution}

The experiment pipeline started by running the configurations on the Qiskit AER simulator to verify the correctness of the code. During the simulation, we found that executing circuits becomes time-consuming (more than one hour) for configurations involving more than 8~qubits. After the simulation, we ran 1\,024 shots per job on two quantum providers and four physical QPUs, \textit{ibm\_fez}, \textit{ibm\_torino}, and \textit{ibm\_marrakesh}, accessible through the IBM Quantum service, and the Rigetti \textit{Ankaa-3} device, accessible through Amazon Web Services (AWS). In IBM backends, each configuration was transpiled at four optimization levels (0 to~3). In the Rigetti backend, the circuits were transpiled at the optimization level~3 using the default provider settings.

\subsection{Variables}

The independent variables of the experiment are the algorithm, the circuit configuration (which determines the target quantum state and the type of experiment), and the quantum backend (for IBM, we also vary the optimization level). The dependent variable is the DSR computed from the histogram returned by each job and the target state. In addition, we record several pre-runtime metrics (e.g.\ circuit depth and number of qubits) to track how the DSR degrades for larger configurations of the algorithms.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the empirical results of 1,060 QPU runs covering jobs based on the Grover, QFT, and vTP algorithms (136, 128, and 796 jobs, respectively). Our analysis covers the DSR degration as a function of qubit count scaling and the circuit depth. We also benchmark DSR against Hellinger and TVD fidelity to highlight how their behaviors complement each other.

\subsection{DSR versus number of qubits}

Figure~\ref{fig:combined_qubits} shows the DSR distribution of each algorithm as a function of the number of qubits, using the optimization level~3 offered by Qiskit.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/1_combined_dsr_comparison.png}
  \caption{DSR versus number of qubits for Grover search, QFT, and vTP at optimization level~3 (IBM and Rigetti backends).}
  \label{fig:combined_qubits}
\end{figure}

Grover's algorithm achieves a median DSR of~0.89 at 3~qubits, drops to~0.68 at 4~qubits, and falls to zero at 6~qubits. The degradation of DSR in the Grover search reflects the exponential growth of the problem space ($2^n$) and the increase in the depth of the circuit required by the oracle and diffusion operators, increasing the accumulative error due to hardware noise. 

QFT degrades more slowly than Grover's search, maintaining a median DSR above~0.80 up to 5~qubits and still reporting~0.28 at 10~qubits. The slower decay is consistent with the shallower circuit structure of the QFT compared with Grover for equivalent qubit counts. 

Finally, as reported in our previous study \cite{vTPMarquez_2026}, vTP remains near zero in all qubit counts (median~$\approx 0.22$), being effective only for payloads involving less than two qubits, which is expected given the dependency of CNOT operations when scaling the transported quantum state.

\subsection{DSR versus depth}

Figure~\ref{fig:combined_depth} presents the DSR distribution grouped by circuit depth bins of 200~gates. To define the bins in this plot, we use all optimization levels, because the data for the three algorithms spans a wide range of circuit depths, and the final transpiled circuits change with the optimization. Therefore, to properly capture the DSR degradation, we chose to include all available data.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/1_combined_dsr_comparison_depth.png}
  \caption{DSR versus depth (binned, 200~gates) for Grover, QFT, and vTP across all optimization levels (IBM and Rigetti backends).}
  \label{fig:combined_depth}
\end{figure}

For circuits with a depth below~200, both Grover and QFT exhibit high median DSR values ($0.88$ and~$0.84$, respectively). For circuit depths exceeding 200 gates, both algorithms exhibit a pronounced degradation in performance. 

The DSR on the QFT algorithm decreases monotonically, reaching 0 in the 400–600 gate interval, followed by a slight recovery in the 600–800 gate interval. The reason for the recovery is associated with the limited number of experiments with a high number of qubits in the round-trip configurations. 

Grover's decline exhibits a non-monotonic pattern. Specifically, the 200--400~bin (median~$0.37$) is lower than the 400--600~bin (median~$0.74$). This inversion occurs because the 200--400 range includes configurations with four marked states (M4-4), producing deeper oracle circuits and lower DSR, whereas the 400--600~bin contains single-marked-state configurations (S4-1) that achieve higher DSR. This observation indicates that the DSR cannot be characterized solely by the circuit depth; rather, the structural complexity of the circuit exerts a significant influence.

\subsection{Effect of optimization level on QFT}

Figure~\ref{fig:qft_heatmap} displays a heatmap of the median DSR of QFT executions as a function of the number of qubits (x-axis) and the optimization level (y-axis).

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/1_combined_dsr_qft_heatmap_optimization.png}
  \caption{Heatmap of median DSR of QFT runs by number of qubits and optimization level (IBM backends). Darker green indicates higher DSR.}
  \label{fig:qft_heatmap}
\end{figure}

At the optimization level~0, the median DSR is low from 2~qubits ($0.38$) and collapses to near zero by 5--6~qubits. At level~1, the DSR improves substantially (e.g., $0.89$ at 3~qubits), but still degrades to~$0.18$ at 10~qubits. The behavior of levels 0 and 1 of optimization is consistent with the expected error accumulation output degradation. The DSR therefore displays lower values as more operations introduce sufficient error to degrade the expected output.

Levels~2 and~3 exhibit comparable performance, where the DSR values above~$0.80$ up to 5~qubits and a gradual decline afterward. The difference between them is small (e.g. $0.56$ vs. \ $0.51$ at 6~qubits), suggesting that the level~3 of optimization provides only a marginal improvement over the level~2.

The heatmap also reveals an anomaly at the optimization level~0 for 7--8~qubits, where the median DSR increases from $0.19$ to $0.45$. This pattern is attributable to specific period-detection and round-trip configurations in which the unoptimized circuit happens to preserve the expected output despite its greater depth, and to the small sample size at those qubit counts (only 1~job at 7~qubits and 2~jobs at 8~qubits, compared with 8~jobs at 6~qubits).

\subsection{Comparison with other fidelity metrics}
\label{sec:metric_comparison}

To position DSR with respect to existing fidelity metrics, we computed Hellinger fidelity and TVD fidelity ($1 - \text{TVD}$) for each QPU run. The process required determining the ideal probability distribution using a statevector simulation, which is feasible only for the circuit sizes in our experiment set. Figures~\ref{fig:grover_metric_comparison} and~\ref{fig:grover_metric_comparison_aws} present the three metrics side by side as a function of the number of qubits for Grover on the IBM and Rigetti backends, respectively.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/1_grover_metric_comparison_ibm.png}
  \caption{DSR, Hellinger fidelity, and TVD fidelity for the Grover algorithm as a function of the number of qubits (IBM backends, optimization levels~2 and~3).}
  \label{fig:grover_metric_comparison}
\end{figure}

For Grover on IBM (Figure~\ref{fig:grover_metric_comparison}), all three metrics are tightly clustered at 2--3~qubits, with medians above~$0.84$. At 4~qubits, DSR exhibits a wider interquartile range ($0.55$--$0.77$) than Hellinger ($0.62$--$0.73$) and TVD ($0.48$--$0.59$) fidelities. This larger spread occurs because DSR averages the expected probability across all marked states ($\bar{p}_{\text{exp}}$). In configurations with multiple marked states (e.g., M4-4 with $K=4$), the probability of each individual state is diluted, making it easier for a competing peak to become dominant, while Hellinger and TVD fidelity (comparing full distributions) are less sensitive to the number of target states. At 5~qubits, DSR decreases to a median of~$0.23$, while Hellinger and TVD fidelities drop to~$0.09$ and~$0.08$, respectively. From 6~qubits onward, all three metrics converge to near zero.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plots/1_grover_metric_comparison_aws.png}
  \caption{DSR, Hellinger fidelity, and TVD fidelity for the Grover algorithm as a function of the number of qubits (Rigetti Ankaa-3 via AWS).}
  \label{fig:grover_metric_comparison_aws}
\end{figure}

For Grover on Rigetti Ankaa-3 (Figure~\ref{fig:grover_metric_comparison_aws}), the three metrics agree at 2~qubits (medians above~$0.90$). However, at 3~qubits DSR collapses to a median of~$0.00$, while Hellinger fidelity retains a median of~$0.25$ and TVD fidelity~$0.20$. The same pattern persists at 4~qubits, where DSR remains at~$0.00$ while Hellinger and TVD report~$0.25$ and~$0.15$, respectively. This discrepancy occurs because the noise level of Ankaa-3 causes the strongest competing peak to match the average probability of the expected state ($\bar{p}_{\text{exp}}$), pushing the DSR to zero. In contrast, Hellinger and TVD fidelity, which evaluate the intersection of the full distribution, still detect partial overlap.

By checking the success score of each metric in both quantum providers (Figures~\ref{fig:grover_metric_comparison} and \ref{fig:grover_metric_comparison_aws}), it is evident that the gate fidelities of Rigetti are lower than those of IBM. In particular, DSR is the first metric to signal complete failure in the noisier backend, confirming its sensitivity to loss of peak distinction. In contrast, Hellinger and TVD fidelity provide a gradual degradation of the signal, which can detect partial algorithm success even when the expected outcome no longer dominates the histogram.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The experimental results (Section~\ref{sec:results}) demonstrate that DSR effectively characterizes the degradation of quantum algorithm outputs as circuits scale in both qubit count and circuit depth. However, as with other fidelity metrics, there exist limitations and important considerations that must be taken into account when determining the appropriate conditions to use DSR.

\textbf{Usability.} The DSR is not intended to replace or compete with existing fidelity metrics, but rather to complement them, with the specific objective of enabling non-experts in QC to determine whether a given quantum algorithm operates as expected.

\textbf{Applicability.} The DSR is designed for algorithms in which the developer can specify a well-defined set of expected outcomes~$E$. This requirement is satisfied by search algorithms (e.g., Grover), basis transformation algorithms (e.g., QFT), and state-preparation protocols (e.g., vTP). However, DSR is not directly applicable to algorithms whose ideal output is a broad probability distribution without a discrete set of dominant states (e.g., variational algorithms). In such cases, distribution-level metrics, such as Hellinger fidelity or TVD, are more appropriate.

\textbf{Confounding factors.} The depth analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:combined_depth}) revealed that circuit depth is not a good predictor of DSR, since different circuit configurations can be mapped to similar depths. This observation suggests that developers using DSR should take into account the configuration complexity of the circuits when creating the analyses---for instance, by restricting the analysis to a single family of configurations (e.g., scalability only) or by normalizing depth relative to the number of qubits.

\textbf{Validity considerations.} There are three main factors that directly affect DSR values. First, a reduced number of shots, particularly when acquired with noisy hardware, introduces sampling uncertainty that propagates into the DSR value. Second, the correctness of the target quantum states~$E$ is essential for an
accurate evaluation. Third, backend calibration drift between jobs may introduce variability not attributable to the algorithm but to hardware fluctuations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we introduce the Differential Success Rate (DSR), a fidelity metric designed to validate the outputs of quantum algorithms. The DSR quantifies how distinctly a predefined set of expected outcomes stands out within the measurement histogram of a quantum job. To this end, the DSR adopts the Michelson contrast formulation to create a scale-invariant score within the interval $[0, 1]$, providing insight into whether a given quantum algorithm performs as expected.

In the initial part of this study, four different formulations are evaluated to identify the one that best captures the performance degradation across quantum executions. The Michelson formulation is compared with the ratio, logarithmic ratio, and normalized margin methods and ultimately demonstrates superior performance, due to its wider dynamic range and its sensitivity to early performance degradation.

To empirically evaluate the capabilities of DSR, we execute DSR on 1,060 quantum jobs on two distinct quantum service providers (IBM Quantum and Rigetti via Amazon Web Services). These experiments include Grover’s search, the Quantum Fourier Transform, and the variation of the teleportation protocol, and are executed on four quantum processing units. The results indicate that DSR accurately captures the degradation in performance, showing scores above~$0.80$ for small circuits, and progressively decreases as the quantum operations present in the algorithm increase. Additionally, we characterize the effect of optimization level by analyzing the mean DSR as a function of qubit count and optimization level. Finally, we compare the DSR with the Hellinger fidelity and the TVD fidelity, revealing that DSR is the first metric to signal complete algorithm failure on noisier hardware, while Hellinger and TVD fidelity provide a more gradual degradation.

In future work, we plan to create automated benchmarking pipelines for continuous monitoring of algorithm performance on different quantum providers and to generate a dashboard to visualize the performance history. We also intend to train neural networks to predict DSR values. Finally, we plan to benchmark the hardware-level gate fidelities with the DSR values to determine whether DSR can serve as a lightweight proxy for circuit-level quality characterization.
